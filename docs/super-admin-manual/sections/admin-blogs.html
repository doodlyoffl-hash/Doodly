Blogs

The DOODLY journal manager — write, schedule and publish the articles that appear on the customer website, with a full Draft → Scheduled → Published ⇄ Archived lifecycle, automatic slug and reading-time, SEO metadata, live view counts, soft-delete/restore, and a one-click preview, all backed by the DOODLY database.

ℹ️
What this screen is, and how it is built

The Blogs item under Admin → Content opens the storefront admin page /admin/blogs.html. That page renders a single block, blogBoard, which mounts into #blogMount and is driven entirely by the live DOODLY database through the API /api/admin/blog. Everything you see — the seven KPI cards, the search box and two dropdowns, the posts table, the New post and Manage forms — is real data. When the backend is reachable a green “● Live” banner confirms it and names the database (for example “● Live — 12 post(s) · 8 published · 3 draft · 1 scheduled · 4,120 views from the DOODLY database”). Published posts (and scheduled posts whose time has arrived) appear on the public customer journal, ordered newest-first, and each page-view they receive is counted back into the Views column here.

ℹ️
Who can view and who can manage

Two permission levels guard this module. Viewing (the board, KPIs and table) is open to any role that can view blogs, reports or CMS — that is Super Admin, Admin and Marketing directly, plus read-only report viewers such as Accountant, Operations, Procurement, Quality and Inventory (through their reports:view access). Managing a post — create, edit, publish/unpublish, duplicate, delete/restore — requires the blogs edit permission, which by default only Super Admin, Admin and Marketing hold. A blocked mutation returns HTTP 403 and the screen shows “Only Marketing / Admin can manage the blog (403)”. The server-side checks canViewBlog / canManageBlog are the real boundary — hidden or greyed buttons are convenience only.

ℹ️
Status is partly derived — you set a state, the system decides the badge

You choose a post’s state (Draft / Published / Scheduled / Archived) in the form, but the coloured status badge on the table is computed from that state together with the schedule and the deletion flag. The rule, in order: deleted → Deleted (red); state ARCHIVED → Archived (grey); state DRAFT → Draft (amber); state SCHEDULED with a future schedule date → Scheduled (blue); state SCHEDULED whose date has already passed → Published (green); anything else → Published. So a post set to Scheduled for next Monday shows as Scheduled until that day and then goes live — and starts appearing on the customer website — on its own, with no manual toggling.

ℹ️
Slug and reading-time are automatic

You never have to calculate two of the fields. The slug (the URL-friendly part of the address, e.g. why-a2-milk) is generated from the title if you leave it blank — lower-cased, spaces and symbols turned to dashes, & turned to “and”, trimmed to 80 characters — and is always made unique by appending -2, -3 and so on if that slug already exists. The reading time is recomputed every time you save from the article body: HTML tags are stripped, words are counted, divided by 200 words-per-minute, and rounded up to at least 1 minute. It shows as “N min read” under the title and in the editor header.

💡
Tip — more lifecycle actions exist on the API than on the form

The form covers everyday editing, but the backend also exposes dedicated schedule and archive actions and a bulk action that can publish, unpublish, archive, delete or restore up to 1,000 posts in one call — available to the API and to any future multi-select tooling. On this screen you achieve the same outcomes through the Status dropdown plus the Publish / Delete buttons.

✅
Best Practice — preview before you publish, and fill the SEO fields

Because the Content field renders raw HTML, always click Preview to confirm the layout, image and formatting look right before you Publish. And spend the extra minute on the collapsible SEO metadata — an SEO title, a description and a handful of keywords make the article far easier to find on search engines than the auto-derived title alone.

ℹ️
Scheduling is “publish on arrival”, not a background job

A Scheduled post does not need a cron to go live. The moment its Schedule for date has passed, the derived status becomes Published and the public feed starts serving it automatically (the feed asks for PUBLISHED posts and SCHEDULED posts whose date is on/before now). Until then the badge stays Scheduled (blue) and the article is invisible to customers.

✅
Best Practice — draft, preview, schedule, then let it launch itself

Write the post as a Draft, use Preview to check it renders correctly, fill in the SEO fields, then set Status to Scheduled with a future date and Save changes. It will publish on its own on that day — no need to remember to come back and flip the switch. Reserve the instant Publish button for time-sensitive news.

⛔
Caution — delete is reversible, but the post disappears from the site immediately

Deleting is a soft delete: the post and its view history are kept and can be Restored. But the instant you delete, the post is pulled from the public journal and hidden from the default board. If you only want to take an article off the site temporarily, prefer Unpublish or Archive over Delete so it stays visible in your working list.

Frequently asked questions

Q: What is the difference between Unpublish, Archive and Delete?

Unpublish sends a live post back to Draft so you can keep editing it; it leaves the website. Archive retires a post (state Archived, grey badge) — a tidy “done with this” state that keeps it in the list. Delete soft-deletes it: hidden from the site and the default board, but recoverable via Restore. All three take the article off the public journal; they differ in intent and where the post then lives in your workflow.

Q: Do I have to write the slug and reading time myself?

No. Leave the Slug blank and it is generated from the title (and made unique automatically). The reading time is always calculated from the Content on save — you never type it.

Q: My post is set to Published but shows the Scheduled badge — why?

That happens when the Status is actually Scheduled with a future date, not Published. The blue Scheduled badge means “not live yet”. It flips to the green Published badge automatically once the schedule date passes, or immediately if you click Publish or clear the schedule.

Q: Can I use images and formatting in the article?

Yes. The Content field supports HTML, and there is a dedicated Featured image URL for the hero image. Because raw HTML is rendered, always Preview before publishing to confirm it looks right.

Q: Where do published posts actually appear?

On the public DOODLY customer website’s journal. The public feed serves posts whose derived status is Published (including scheduled posts whose date has arrived), newest first. Each time a reader opens an article its Views counter goes up, which feeds the Views column and the Total Views KPI here.

Q: Does duplicating a post copy its views and publish it?

No. A duplicate is created as a fresh Draft named “… (copy)” with a new “…-copy” slug, its view count reset to zero and no publish date. It never touches the original — it is a safe starting point for a similar article.
